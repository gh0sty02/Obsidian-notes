A couple of recruiters posted the 7 Brutal honest job search tips that work in 2026 but no one is talking about it. **These tips got 100s of upvotes** and provided a refreshing insight into 2026 job search market and what is working best.

Sharing some tips and best actionable advise for job search

#### Tip 1: Never use Easy Apply — here’s why it hurts you:

When you hit “Easy Apply” on LinkedIn or Indeed, your resume doesn’t go straight to the recruiter. These platforms batch and send applications to company systems every few hours — meaning even if you’re the very first person to apply, you could be sitting in a queue while others who went directly to the careers page are already being reviewed.

On top of that, some companies don’t even have a system that pulls from LinkedIn or Indeed automatically. Recruiters have to manually check each platform — and sometimes the feeds break entirely and applications never arrive at all.

**The fix is simple: always go to the company’s careers page and apply directly.** It’s faster, more reliable, and puts your resume in front of the recruiter without the middleman.

#### Tip 2: If the job asks for 3–5 years of experience, apply with 0–2 anyway.

Those numbers are usually placeholders — someone filled in a range because the job posting required it, not because it’s a hard cutoff.

If your resume clearly shows you can do the work, years of experience matter a lot less than people think. A strong resume with real proof of ability will outperform a longer resume with nothing compelling to show.

**The worst they can say is no. Apply anyway.**

#### Tip 3: Got rejected instantly after applying? It’s probably knockout questions.

Most applications have automatic filters that screen out candidates before a human ever sees the resume. Things like location, required software experience, or compensation expectations — if your answer doesn’t match what the company needs, the system removes you automatically.

Some of these filters are legitimate. If a role can only be filled in a specific city, there’s no point moving you forward. But others are too aggressive — rejecting someone because their salary expectation is slightly over budget, for example, when a quick conversation could’ve sorted that out.

If you’re getting rejected immediately and consistently, go back and review how you’re answering those screening questions. That’s usually where it’s happening.

#### Tip 4: Use AI wisely for resume and cover letters are mostly optional.

**Recruiters can tell when a resume was AI-generated, but most don’t care.** What they do care about is whether they can quickly understand your career and the impact you’ve had.

Where AI resumes fall apart is when they’re full of buzzwords and generic responsibilities with nothing concrete behind them. If your resume just mirrors the job description or lists what your role was supposed to do, that’s a red flag. But if it clearly shows what you actually achieved — with real numbers, outcomes, and results tied to the role you’re applying for — you’re in good shape.

**The rule is simple: AI can help you write it, but your impact has to be in there.**

**Not every recruiter reads cover letter — plenty don't at all.** But some hiring managers genuinely value them, so it depends on who's on the other side. If the application requires one, use AI to put something together quickly and move on. If it's optional, it's probably not worth the time.

#### Tip 5: Apply within 24 hours of a job being posted — especially for competitive roles.

Most job postings get 200+ applications within the first day. How quickly a recruiter reviews those apps depends on the role — for high-volume or ongoing positions, they might be checking daily. For a single-hire role, they may only review once a week until their interview calendar fills up.

Either way, being early puts you near the top of the pile before it gets crowded. The longer you wait, the more buried your application gets — and some recruiters stop reviewing altogether once they have enough strong candidates to move forward.

**When you see a role you want, apply the same day.**

#### Tip 6: Your LinkedIn headline works harder than your resume — if you write it right.

Recruiters search for candidates using keywords, and your headline is one of the first things that gets scanned. A headline like “Marketing Manager | SaaS | Paid Media” shows up in those searches. “Marketing Manager at Company X” doesn’t.

Your current title and employer tell recruiters where you’ve been. Keywords in your headline tell them what you can do — and that’s what gets you found.

#### Tip 7: Connect with Agencies that can help you get hired. However, be cautious and don’t pay anyone upfront.

Recruiters work closely with agents for a lot of roles for which the requisition is never opened publicly. **However, most important thing is to be cautious. Never pay any money upfront. The best to agree to is to work with agencies that will take a cut of your pay once you land the job. Be careful also while signing contract and review with care.**
